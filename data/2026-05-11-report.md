## This week's highlights

The five most useful findings for product/positioning work:

1. **Mindbody contract lock-in intensifying — BBB complaints quote Mindbody enforcing contracts through 2026.** "Bait and switch company taking advantage of small businesses... Do not sign any kind of contract with them because you will absolutely not be able to get out." Customer who tried to cancel within hours was denied. Source: [BBB](https://www.bbb.org/us/ca/san-luis-obispo/profile/computer-software-developers/mindbody-inc-1236-5002899/complaints).

2. **Acuity support is the new sharp grievance.** "I loved this booking system...until something went wrong and I needed customer support. Help is pretty much non-existent. No phone number to call. Just fill out an online form and submit." (Feb 14, 2026.) Plus a "really confusing... buried in weird places" UX complaint. Source: [Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/acuityscheduling.com), [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/191978/Acuity-Scheduling/reviews/).

3. **Fresha is the new MassageBook on marketplace fees — and the QR-code edge case is the smoking gun.** "I'm being charged for customers who scan my in-store QR code to book." Massage therapists are paying "new client" fees for clients they themselves brought in. Pattern echoes last week's MassageBook directory-fee complaints. Source: [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/142138/Shedul-com/reviews/).

4. **Jane charting setup is too heavy for non-clinical solos — even loyal users churn.** "Really loved it except for the lack of simplicity in charting templates... as someone that is more easily overwhelmed, it just wasn't a right fit for me." This validates the exact wedge Roomwell can hit: out-of-box SOAP notes that don't require template-engineering. Source: [Capterra page 8](https://www.capterra.com/p/178984/Jane-App/reviews/?page=8).

5. **ClinicSense mobile app is a fresh, named weakness.** "I currently download an icon of the desktop version to my phone for a shortcut to the calendar instead because the phone app just feels choppy and is annoying to navigate." Plus a separate user: "The app is usable on the iPad and laptop but definitely not on mobile phone." If Roomwell ships a strong mobile-first experience, this is a quotable wedge against the leading all-in-one solo competitor. Source: [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/178722/ClinicSense/reviews/).

## Cost complaints

### Mindbody
- "Bait and switch company taking advantage of small businesses with little to no transparency about their program without actually signing up and paying ridiculous fees. Then will not allow you to cancel (one day later) when you finally enter the program... Do not sign any kind of contract with them because you will absolutely not be able to get out." — [BBB](https://www.bbb.org/us/ca/san-luis-obispo/profile/computer-software-developers/mindbody-inc-1236-5002899/complaints)
- "Businesses are expected to sign a 12-month contract and commit $2k – $7k of their revenue to Mindbody." — [Pabau pricing analysis](https://pabau.com/blog/mindbody-pricing/). First concrete commitment-floor range we've captured. Monthly plans range from $139 (Starter) to $599 (Ultimate Plus).

### Fresha
- "I'm being charged for customers who scan my in-store QR code to book" — massage therapist, [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/142138/Shedul-com/reviews/). Fresha's free-platform-with-marketplace-fees model has the same trust-erosion pattern as MassageBook directory fees.

### Jane
- "At $54-139/month, it's expensive for what is essentially scheduling with clinical add-ons. If you don't need charting or insurance billing, a more affordable scheduling platform will serve you better." — [EHR Insider review](https://ehrinsider.com/reviews/jane-app/). The reviewer is essentially writing Roomwell's segmentation in plain English.

### Noterro
- "Cost was far too high while trying to start a business and didn't like that essential features would require them to increase their subscription" — [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/164994/SOAP-Vault/reviews/). First Noterro cost complaint we've captured — tier-upgrade creep exists at Noterro too, contrary to last week's uniformly-positive picture.

### Vagaro
- "If you cancel your account or deactivate credit card processing within 12 months of ordering the card reader, you will be charged the price of the reader" — [Vagaro Support](https://support.vagaro.com/hc/en-us/articles/360000192774-Order-Your-Free-Credit-Card-Reader-and-Discounted-Hardware). Official policy explaining the recurring $140 "free card reader" clawback complaints captured last week. Now we have receipts on the structure, not just the gripes.

### Square Appointments
- "Calendar sync would cost $40 per month as part of the Plus plan—a significant expense for solo operators who only need this specific feature" — [Square Community](https://community.squareup.com/t5/Payments-Troubleshooting/Appointments-pricing-calendar-sync-should-be-free/m-p/631595). Same complaint as last week's Square Community thread, surfaced in a separate forum location — confirms the pattern is widespread, not single-thread.

No new findings this week for Acuity, MassageBook, ClinicSense, or Schedulicity in the cost theme.

## Switching activity

**Direction of flow this week (where signal exists):**

- **Mindbody → out (forcibly retained).** The dominant switching story this week is *attempted* switching that Mindbody is blocking via contract enforcement. BBB complaints describe customers locked into contracts "through September 11, 2026" and beyond, even after one-day cancellation attempts. This is less "where they're switching to" and more "where they're stuck." Source: [BBB](https://www.bbb.org/us/ca/san-luis-obispo/profile/computer-software-developers/mindbody-inc-1236-5002899/complaints).
- **Paper/Google Calendar → Jane.** "As my practice grew, that informal system became increasingly unsustainable. Jane offered everything I needed in one dedicated platform" — solo LMT switching from informal tools straight to Jane, [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/178984/Jane-App/reviews/). Notable: first-time-software solos are not picking laterally from MassageBook/ClinicSense — they go upmarket to Jane. Worth a follow-up: is this because Jane has better SEO, vendor reputation, or LMT word-of-mouth?
- **Jane → ??? (churn signal, no named destination).** Solo churning because Jane's charting templates were too overwhelming. Destination unnamed in the review — but the wedge is clear: a more opinionated, less-configurable charting experience.

**Real competitive set for Roomwell — refined from last week:**
- **Upmarket boundary remains Jane.** Validated by the cost reviewer who said "if you don't need charting or insurance billing, a more affordable scheduling platform will serve you better."
- **Direct competitors remain Noterro, ClinicSense, MassageBook.** New this week: ClinicSense's mobile-app gap and first negative support signal both crack a previously uniformly-positive picture. Noterro also picked up its first cost and support complaints this week.
- **Fresha emerges as an indirect threat.** Free entry tier + massage-therapist marketplace presence pulls solos in, then the new-client fees create the same trust friction as MassageBook directory fees. Track this. If Fresha is acquiring solo LMTs through its free tier and then losing them, that's a switching pool Roomwell could capture.

## Solo practitioner advice threads

This week's strongest verbatim recommendations and the reasoning behind each:

**MassageBook (this week's most quotable persona match):**
- "I am a solo massage therapist, wife and mother of 2. There's is NO way I could make my business work without the online scheduling, appointment reminders, reporting and SOAP notes. It has let me stick with what I do best- Massage! And the rest I do with MassageBook." — [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/147523/MassageBook/reviews/). This is the Maya persona almost word-for-word: solo LMT who frames the software as work-life-enabling, not feature-impressive. "Stick with what I do best — Massage" is the emotional register Roomwell's copy should match.

**Jane (clinical-grade solo segment):**
- "Jane App fulfilled all the requirements for me to run a remote clinic as a solo practitioner. Excellent service, a complete suite of practical business and administration tools." — RMT, [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/178984/Jane-App/reviews/). 5/5. Captures the segment that won't downshift to Roomwell — clinical-grade solo practice that values the depth.

**Noterro (cost-conscious solo startup):**
- "Best decision" ... "affordable, durable, compatible, and extremely useful product that makes the hard job of sole proprietorship much easier" — [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/164994/SOAP-Vault/reviews/). Still the strongest new-solo-startup recommendation pattern.

**Acuity (good-enough-with-caveats):**
- "It works for me as a solo practitioner" — [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/191978/Acuity-Scheduling/reviews/). Tepid endorsement; user wishes Acuity had a waitlist.

**Fresha (positive customer-side, problematic operator-side):**
- "Fresha is easy to use as a customer, including booking, modifying appointments, getting reminder notifications, and providing customer support" — hairdresser, April 8 2026, [Capterra](https://www.capterra.com/p/142138/Shedul-com/reviews/). Captures the front-end experience that hooks solos in, before the marketplace-fee surprise.

**Reasoning patterns this week:**
- Solos at the *clinical-grade-needs* end accept Jane's price for its depth.
- Solos at the *just-starting-out* end gravitate to Noterro for affordability + SOAP.
- The midfield (low-clinical-need solos who don't want to learn templates) is the underserved segment — it's where Jane's "too overwhelmed" churners are landing nowhere obvious, and where MassageBook's emotional positioning ("stick with what I do best") is the strongest existing match.

- **Fresha as an indirect threat via QR-code marketplace fee.** Not on the radar last week. Same trust-friction pattern as MassageBook directory fees, but Fresha is faster-growing and has stronger consumer-facing brand. Treat as a watch-list competitor going forward.

Small sample, but worth tracking — if the underlying companies are scaling and support quality is degrading, that's a window.
